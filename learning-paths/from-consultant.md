# From Consultant to Engineering Manager

Consultants have a unique path to engineering management. You bring structured problem-solving, executive communication, and organizational awareness. The main gaps are direct people management, technical credibility, and engineering culture experience. The transition requires building these skills while leveraging your existing strengths in stakeholder management and strategic thinking.


## What You Already Have

- Structured problem-solving and frameworks
- Client management and stakeholder communication
- Executive presence and presentation skills
- Cross-functional project management
- Market research and competitive analysis
- Organizational change management
- Financial modeling and business case development


## What You Need to Learn

### People Management

- 1:1s, coaching, and career development - the core of the EM role
- Performance management - reviews, calibration, PIPs, promotion advocacy
- Hiring engineers - interview loop design, technical assessment, closing
- Conflict resolution - mediating engineering disagreements, having hard conversations
- Team building - creating psychological safety, building culture, retaining talent

### Technical Credibility

Two paths to choose from:

Path One: Technical Breadth (recommended for most consultants)
- System design fundamentals - architecture patterns, trade-offs, component boundaries
- How engineering teams work - agile/scrum, CI/CD, code review practices, testing
- Pros: immediately applicable, gives you enough depth to participate in architecture discussions
- Fit: those targeting people-first EM roles at product companies

Path Two: Technical Depth (for infrastructure or platform EM roles)
- Hands-on coding in one language
- Database concepts and data modeling
- Cloud infrastructure (AWS, GCP, Azure)
- Pros: deep credibility with engineers, enables better technical judgment
- Fit: those targeting technical EM roles at infrastructure companies

### Engineering Culture

- Code review practices and quality standards
- Testing and CI/CD pipelines
- On-call and incident response
- Engineering career ladders and growth paths


## Why This Transition Works

Consulting teaches you to structure ambiguous problems, communicate with senior stakeholders, and drive organizational change. These are core EM skills. The gap is that consulting recommendations end at the slide deck - EM work starts there. You need to learn how to build and grow a team, earn technical credibility, and drive ongoing execution rather than project-based engagements.


## Suggested Path

1. Build technical literacy - study system design, understand how engineering teams ship software
2. Learn people management fundamentals - read The Making of a Manager, The Manager's Path
3. Practice coaching and feedback - mentor engineers, give direct feedback, run career conversations
4. Join a technical team in a leadership capacity - product ops, TPM, or engineering lead role as a stepping stone
5. Own a hiring process - design interview loops, calibrate rubrics, close candidates
6. Build engineering culture awareness - participate in code reviews, retros, and incident response
7. Deploy your consulting skills - stakeholder management, organizational design, executive communication

For a structured approach to EM interviews with 200+ real examples, check out [The 0→1 EM Interview Playbook](https://valenx.org/books/em).


## Timeline

3-5 months to become interview-ready if you focus on people management and technical literacy first. You already have the strategic and communication foundation - the main work is building people leadership skills and technical credibility on top of that.


## Your Advantage

Structured thinkers with strong communication skills are always in demand for EM roles. You can take an ambiguous organizational problem, break it into actionable plans, and present recommendations to leadership. Most EMs coming from IC backgrounds struggle with executive communication and organizational design. This makes you uniquely valuable, especially at companies navigating complex growth, reorgs, or cross-team coordination challenges.
