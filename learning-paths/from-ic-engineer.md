# From IC Engineer to Engineering Manager

IC engineers have the most common and natural transition to engineering management. EM is primarily a people leadership and delivery role, not a continuation of IC engineering at a higher level. Most of the work is growing engineers, hiring well, and shipping through others. IC engineers already know how products are built, what is technically feasible, and how to ship. The flavor differs - writing code vs. growing people who write code - but the systems thinking transfers directly.


## What You Already Have

- Technical depth - you understand what is hard and what is easy to build
- Shipping experience - you know what it takes to go from idea to production
- Engineering credibility - your team will trust your judgment on technical decisions
- Code review and quality intuition - you can evaluate engineering work effectively
- System understanding - you can reason about architecture trade-offs
- Debugging and root cause analysis - critical for incident management as an EM
- On-call experience - you understand operational burden and engineer wellbeing


## What You Need to Learn

- People management - 1:1s, feedback, performance reviews, career development
- Hiring - interview loop design, rubric calibration, sourcing, closing candidates
- Conflict resolution - mediating disagreements, having difficult conversations
- Stakeholder management - working with product, design, leadership
- Delivery management - sprint planning, risk identification, cross-team coordination
- Letting go of code - your job is no longer writing the best code, it is building the best team


## Why This Transition Works

Technical credibility is permanently relevant in the EM role. Without understanding what is technically feasible, you cannot make good management decisions - you cannot evaluate estimates, guide architecture, or earn trust from your team. Engineers who move into EM roles earn credibility on day one that other managers spend months building.

You can transition by taking on informal leadership responsibilities first - mentoring junior engineers, leading a project, running a hiring loop. Because you understand the engineering work deeply, you can provide meaningful coaching and feedback immediately.


## Suggested Path

1. Start mentoring - take on a junior engineer and practice coaching, feedback, and career conversations
2. Lead a project - practice delivery management, cross-functional communication, and stakeholder updates
3. Learn people management - read The Making of a Manager (Julie Zhuo) and The Manager's Path (Camille Fournier)
4. Run hiring loops - volunteer to design and run interview panels, practice rubric calibration
5. Practice difficult conversations - give direct feedback, address performance issues, mediate conflicts
6. Build cross-functional skills - partner with product and design, practice translating between engineering and business


## Timeline

3-6 months of focused learning on people leadership, hiring, and delivery management. The technical credibility is already there - the main learning curve is shifting from individual contribution to team contribution.


## Your Advantage

You understand the work your team does at a deep level. When an engineer says "this will take 3 weeks," you can have an informed conversation about scope and approach. When architecture trade-offs need to be made, you can participate meaningfully. Engineering teams trust you faster because you speak their language and have been in their position.

The challenge: learning to let go. Your instinct will be to solve problems yourself because you can do it faster. The job is to build a team that can solve problems without you.
