# From Tech Lead to Engineering Manager

Tech leads have the most natural transition to engineering management. You already lead through influence, mentor engineers, and make architectural decisions. The shift is from leading through technical direction to leading through people management and organizational design.


## What You Already Have

- Technical leadership - you set technical direction and guide architecture decisions
- Mentoring experience - you already coach engineers and review their work
- Cross-functional awareness - you work with product, design, and other teams
- Project leadership - you plan and deliver complex technical projects
- Engineering credibility - your team respects your technical judgment
- Design review skills - you evaluate technical proposals and provide feedback
- Code quality standards - you establish and maintain engineering best practices


## What You Need to Learn

- Formal people management - performance reviews, calibration, promotion advocacy, PIPs
- Hiring at scale - interview loop design, rubric creation, sourcing, closing candidates
- Career development - building growth plans, creating stretch assignments, sponsoring promotions
- Organizational design - team structuring, charter definition, reorg planning
- Budget and headcount - capacity modeling, business case building, resource allocation
- Letting go of technical ownership - your job shifts from owning the architecture to empowering someone else to own it


## Why This Transition Works

The overlap between tech lead and EM is significant. Both roles require influence without authority, mentoring, and cross-functional communication. The main additions are formal people management (performance reviews, hiring, career development) and organizational thinking (team structure, headcount, budget).

Most tech leads have already been doing informal management - coaching, running retros, resolving conflicts. The transition formalizes these responsibilities and adds the administrative side of people leadership.


## Suggested Path

1. Take on 1:1 responsibilities - run regular 1:1s with 2-3 engineers, practice coaching and feedback
2. Own a hiring loop - design interview rounds, calibrate rubrics, run debriefs
3. Practice performance conversations - write mock reviews, give direct feedback, handle difficult situations
4. Learn career development - study career ladders, build growth plans for engineers you mentor
5. Study organizational design - read An Elegant Puzzle (Will Larson), understand team topologies and Conway's Law
6. Shift your identity - from "the best technical decision-maker" to "the person who grows the best technical decision-makers"


## Timeline

2-4 months. You already have the hardest-to-learn parts: technical credibility, mentoring skills, and cross-functional awareness. The main learning is on the formal people management side - performance reviews, hiring processes, and career development.


## Your Advantage

You understand engineering leadership at a deep level. You can evaluate technical proposals, guide architecture decisions, and maintain engineering quality while adding the people management layer. Many first-time EMs coming from IC backgrounds struggle with the technical leadership side because they were individual contributors, not leaders. You have already made that first leap.

Your challenge: letting go of being the primary technical decision-maker. As an EM, your job is to develop the next generation of tech leads - people who can make the same quality decisions you would, without you in the room.
