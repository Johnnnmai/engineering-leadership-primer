# Engineering Manager Role: Reality vs. Job Postings

Based on real discussions from Reddit, Blind, and X (assessed via deep research with ChatGPT, Gemini, Grok) and conversations with hiring managers and candidates shared with me privately. Complements the quantitative job analysis with qualitative insights from what people actually report once they are in the role.

From an engineering director I spoke with: "hired for 'Engineering Manager' roles three times. the job description said technical leadership and team building. the job turned out to be 60% project management + status meetings + escalation handling. the 980 JDs probably have the same problem - they describe ambition, not what you do on Monday morning"


## Engineering Manager vs Tech Lead vs Director: The Confusion

Problem: Titles are misaligned. Recruiters post "Engineering Manager" but mean Tech Lead with reports, or companies hire an EM but need a Director.

Consensus from discussions:
- EMs focus on people and delivery
- Tech Leads focus on technical direction and architecture
- Directors focus on strategy, org design, and multi-team alignment
- "Engineering Manager" is often a catch-all title driven by company growth

### What People Actually Report

- Applying for Engineering Manager -> Get grilled on system design and coding
- Expecting people leadership -> Asked to manage project timelines and write status reports
- Wanting to grow engineers -> Evaluated on delivery velocity and OKR metrics


## What Engineering Managers Actually Do


### Three Primary Work Types

People Leaders (~50%): Hiring, coaching, performance, retention
- Running interview loops across multiple open reqs simultaneously
- Writing performance reviews for 8-12 direct reports each cycle
- Having difficult conversations about underperformance
- Coaching engineers through career transitions (IC to lead, mid to senior)
- Building team culture through 1:1s, retros, and team norms

Delivery Drivers (~35%): Project execution, coordination, stakeholder management
- Running sprint planning and ensuring predictable delivery
- Managing cross-team dependencies for complex projects
- Communicating status and risks to product and leadership
- Scope negotiation when timelines are at risk
- Post-launch retrospectives and continuous improvement

Technical Stewards (~15%): Architecture guidance, tech debt, quality
- Participating in design reviews and architecture decisions
- Prioritizing tech debt reduction alongside feature work
- Setting engineering standards and code review norms
- Managing on-call rotations and incident response
- Evaluating build vs buy decisions for infrastructure

### Day-to-Day vs. Assumptions

- "Lead technical direction" -> Reviewing PRDs and negotiating scope with product
- "Build the team" -> Spending 10+ hours per week in interview loops during hiring sprints
- "Drive engineering excellence" -> Running calibration sessions and writing performance reviews
- "Strategic technical leadership" -> Context-switching between 1:1s, stand-ups, cross-functional syncs, and status reports

### The Decision Loop

Real challenges people report:
- Navigating the tension between shipping fast and maintaining code quality
- Handling an underperformer while keeping team morale intact
- Advocating for headcount when leadership is focused on cost reduction
- Deciding whether to be in the weeds on a critical project or trust the team


## Jobs vs. Reality Gap

| Dimension | Job Postings Say | People Actually Experience |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------------|
| Technical depth | "Strong technical background" | Rarely writing code; architecture guidance and code reviews |
| People management | Mentioned in 76.3% | Dominates the day-to-day; more time in 1:1s than in design reviews |
| Hiring | "Build world-class teams" | 10-15 hours/week during hiring sprints, intense and draining |
| Delivery | Often understated | Major time consumer; status reports, stakeholder management, risk mitigation |
| Strategic work | "Technical vision and strategy" | Happens quarterly at best; daily reality is tactical execution |


## The "Combo Role" Problem


Common complaint: Jobs demand 3 roles in 1.

Reality: "Engineering Manager" often means:
- Tech Lead (architecture decisions and code reviews)
- Project Manager (delivery timelines and stakeholder communication)
- People Manager (hiring, performance, career development)
- Or actual Engineering Management (all three balanced appropriately)


## Key Takeaways

1. Titles are broken: "Engineering Manager" means different things at different companies
2. People leadership wins: Hiring, coaching, and retention are the actual differentiators
3. Delivery management is underrated: Project execution consumes more time than most candidates expect
4. Technical depth serves leadership: You need enough depth to maintain credibility, not to be the best coder
5. Gap remains: Job postings do not fully reflect what is actually tested or experienced day-to-day
