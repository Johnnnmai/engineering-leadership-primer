# Engineering Management Jobs Analysis

Generated from 980+ job descriptions extracted from major job boards.

I searched for jobs containing "Engineering Manager" keyword from LA, NY, London, Amsterdam, Berlin, and the Bay Area for the last 4 weeks. I did it at the beginning of February 2026, so it mostly contains jobs published in January 2026.

Summary

- 62% of roles focus on people-first leadership (hiring, coaching, performance, team health)
- 88.7% need skills beyond just technical leadership - it is a people and delivery role
- 76.3% of roles mention hiring and talent development - the most common skill across all jobs
- 58.7% of EM roles require architectural judgment and technical depth
- Enterprise/Platform teams (412 jobs) > Product teams (298 jobs) > Infrastructure teams (270 jobs)


## "Engineering Manager" Job Types

Job positions I analyzed fall into these categories:

- People-First
- Delivery-First
- Technical EM

### People-First: 608 jobs (62.1%)

Leading, growing, and retaining engineering teams.

What they own:

- Hiring and interview loop design
- Performance management and career development
- Team health, engagement, and retention
- Coaching and mentoring engineers from junior to senior
- Creating engineering culture and team norms

Example responsibilities:

- "Recruit, hire, and develop a world-class engineering team"
- "Conduct performance reviews and provide ongoing career coaching"
- "Build an inclusive team culture that attracts and retains top talent"
- "Own the engineer hiring pipeline from sourcing to close"
- "Manage a team of 8-12 engineers and drive their professional growth"

Position title examples

- Engineering Manager
- Senior Engineering Manager
- Engineering Manager II
- Head of Engineering
- Software Engineering Manager
- Principal Engineering Manager


### Delivery-First: 292 jobs (29.8%)

Driving project delivery and cross-team coordination alongside people management.

These roles blend delivery execution with team leadership. The emphasis is on shipping rather than pure people development.

What they own:

- Sprint planning and delivery execution
- Cross-team dependency management
- Technical project planning and risk mitigation
- Stakeholder communication and status reporting
- Release management and operational readiness

Example responsibilities:

- "Drive on-time delivery of quarterly roadmap commitments"
- "Coordinate cross-functional workstreams across 3+ teams"
- "Own the sprint process and ensure predictable delivery velocity"
- "Manage project timelines, identify risks, and communicate status to leadership"
- "Partner with product to translate requirements into engineering plans"

Position title examples

- Engineering Manager - Delivery
- Software Development Manager
- Engineering Lead
- Development Manager
- Engineering Manager - Platform


### Technical EM: 80 jobs (8.1%)

Deep technical ownership with management responsibilities.

What they own:

- System architecture decisions
- Technical debt strategy and prioritization
- Platform reliability and performance
- Engineering standards and best practices
- Code review processes and technical quality

Position title examples

- Engineering Manager - Infrastructure
- Engineering Manager - Platform
- Engineering Manager - ML Systems
- Engineering Manager - Core Services

These "Engineering Manager" roles are traditional staff/principal engineer roles with people management added. They do deep technical work (architecture, reliability, infrastructure) with core people management components.


### How to Tell the Difference

The key question is: Does this role lead through people, or lead through technical decisions?

People-First:

- Owns hiring and team building
- Conducts performance reviews
- Drives career development
- Creates team culture
- Coaches and mentors engineers

Delivery-First:

- Drives project execution
- Manages cross-team dependencies
- Owns delivery timelines
- Coordinates with product and design
- Focuses on sprint process and velocity

Technical EM:

- Owns system architecture
- Sets technical standards
- Manages technical debt strategy
- Deep engineering contribution
- Combines IC depth with people management

## How Much Technical Depth Do EMs Need?

58.7% of EM roles require some level of technical depth

Most common technical expectations:

- Architecture review participation - 285 jobs (29.1%)
- Technical debt prioritization - 234 jobs (23.9%)
- Code review oversight - 212 jobs (21.6%)
- System design interview capability - 198 jobs (20.2%)
- On-call and incident management - 178 jobs (18.2%)

Key findings:

1. Most EMs need architectural judgment - 58.7% require technical depth
2. Hands-on coding is not the norm - only 8.1% focus on writing code as a primary responsibility
3. The most common technical EM skill is architecture review - understanding trade-offs without writing the implementation
4. Technical depth serves people leadership - EMs who understand the code earn trust faster and give better career guidance

Bottom Line: EMs need enough technical depth to make architectural decisions, guide technical strategy, and maintain credibility with their teams. You do not need to be the best coder on the team - you need to be the best leader of coders on the team.


## Key Insight: People + Delivery = 80%+ of EM Scenarios

The two dominant disciplines are:

- People - grow engineers, hire well, retain talent, build culture
- Delivery - ship outcomes, manage dependencies, communicate with stakeholders

If you learn these two disciplines deeply, you can handle most engineering management scenarios.


## Learning Path for Engineering Managers

- Foundation - People leadership basics, active listening, feedback models (SBI, radical candor)
- Hiring - Interview loop design, rubric calibration, sourcing strategy, closing candidates
- Performance - Review writing, calibration sessions, managing underperformers, promotion advocacy
- Delivery - Agile execution, risk management, dependency tracking, stakeholder communication
- Technical Strategy - Architecture reviews, tech debt management, platform decisions
- Organizational - Team structuring, process design, cross-team alignment, budget planning

The typical engineering management stack:

- PEOPLE layer - Hiring, coaching, performance management, career development, retention
- DELIVERY layer - Sprint planning, project execution, cross-team coordination, risk management
- TECHNICAL layer - Architecture guidance, tech debt strategy, operational excellence, security
- STRATEGIC layer - Roadmap ownership, org design, budget planning, executive communication
