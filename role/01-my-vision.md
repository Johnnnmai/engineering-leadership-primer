# My Vision of the Engineering Manager Role

Based on my experience as an Amazon AI/Robotics Lead PM, working closely with engineering managers across Google, Amazon, Meta, and Apple.

This is my personal vision of the role. To see how it compares with what companies actually look for, I analyzed 980+ job descriptions - the rest of this [role section](README.md) is based on that research.


## Core Responsibility

- Owning the team end-to-end: from hiring the right people to shipping the right outcomes
- Growing engineers from junior to senior and beyond through coaching, feedback, and career sponsorship
- Partnering with product, design, and data teams through every phase of the planning to delivery lifecycle
- Not "just make sure the team ships on time" - starts from building a healthy team that delivers consistently
- Creating an environment where engineers do their best work and want to stay


## Beyond "Just Manage the Team"

Even for a straightforward team of 6-8 engineers, professional engineering management requires:

1. Hiring and onboarding - sourcing, phone screens, designing interview loops, calibrating rubrics, ramping new hires
2. Performance management - setting clear expectations, writing reviews, calibrating ratings, having hard conversations
3. Career development - mapping growth paths, creating stretch opportunities, providing sponsorship and visibility
4. Team health - running retrospectives, monitoring engagement signals, addressing burnout, resolving interpersonal conflicts
5. Technical strategy - partnering on architecture decisions, managing tech debt budget, ensuring operational excellence
6. Delivery execution - sprint planning, risk identification, dependency management, stakeholder communication
7. Cross-functional partnership - aligning with product on roadmap, design on UX, data on metrics, leadership on priorities
8. Organizational design - scoping team charters, managing handoffs between teams, proposing reorgs when needed
9. Incident response - on-call culture, post-mortem processes, reliability standards, escalation paths
10. Culture and standards - code review norms, documentation expectations, engineering principles, knowledge sharing


## Progressive Complexity

- Simple case: managing one team of 5-7 engineers on a single product area
- Multi-team management (~5x harder): add cross-team coordination, shared infrastructure decisions, manager-of-managers dynamics, headcount planning
- Organizational leadership (~10x harder): add executive alignment, multi-quarter technical strategy, org design, budget ownership, hiring pipeline at scale


## How It Compares to Other Roles

### vs Tech Lead

- Tech leads own technical direction; EMs own team health and delivery
- Tech leads influence through code and architecture; EMs influence through people and process
- Most common collaboration model: Tech Lead owns the "how," EM owns the "who" and "when"
- Easiest transition path to engineering management
- Tech leads need to add: hiring loops, performance reviews, career coaching, stakeholder management

### vs Director of Engineering

- Directors manage multiple teams and their managers, EMs manage individual contributors directly
- Directors focus on strategy, org design, and cross-org alignment; EMs focus on team-level execution
- EMs handle the day-to-day; Directors handle the quarter-to-quarter
- Directors need organizational design skills, budget management, and executive communication at a higher level

### vs VP of Engineering

- VPs own engineering culture and strategy across the entire organization
- EMs own execution within their team; VPs own outcomes across all teams
- VPs negotiate headcount, set company-wide engineering standards, and represent engineering to the board
- VPs need to add: company-level strategy, board communication, P&L awareness, and talent brand building

### vs TPM (Technical Program Manager)

- TPMs own cross-team coordination and program execution; EMs own team health and delivery
- TPMs do not have direct reports; EMs are responsible for people development
- Overlap happens in delivery management - both track timelines and dependencies
- TPMs need to add: people management, hiring, performance reviews, team building

### What Engineering Managers Don't Do

- Write production code daily (though some maintain technical depth through code reviews and architecture guidance)
- Own the product roadmap (that is the PM's domain)
- Make all technical decisions unilaterally (that is the team's domain, with EM providing guardrails)

### What Engineering Managers Focus On

- Growing engineers through coaching, mentoring, and career development
- Hiring and retaining top talent
- Removing blockers and creating clarity for the team
- Ensuring technical quality without being the sole technical decision-maker
- Partnering with product to translate strategy into executable plans


## In Bigger Organizations

- Engineering responsibilities often split between EMs, Tech Leads, Directors, and VPs
- Tech Leads: technical direction, architecture, code quality
- EMs: team health, hiring, performance, delivery
- Directors: multi-team strategy, org design, budget
- VPs: engineering culture, executive alignment, company-wide technical strategy
- Eventually dedicated EMs own each team, with senior ICs or Tech Leads providing technical depth
- Not every team problem needs an EM - some are pure technical or process problems
