## Key Takeaways

### What is an "Engineering Manager" in 2026?

- It is a role that has evolved significantly. Engineering managers own the intersection of people leadership, technical strategy, and delivery execution. But titles are broken - "Engineering Manager" means different things at different companies.
- Three types of roles hide under the same title:
  - People-First (62.1%) - owns hiring, coaching, performance, team health, and career growth
  - Delivery-First (29.8%) - owns sprint execution, project delivery, and cross-team coordination with some people management
  - Technical EM (8.1%) - deep technical ownership rebranded as EM, closer to a staff engineer with reports

- It is fundamentally a people leadership role. 88.7% of roles need skills beyond technical leadership. Only 5.2% expect pure technical lead work. You need hiring expertise, performance management, stakeholder alignment, and often product partnership too.

### What they actually do

- People management and delivery execution dominate. Hiring, coaching, and performance reviews appear in 76.3% of all jobs, technical roadmap ownership in 41.2%. Together they cover 80%+ of EM scenarios. If you learn these two disciplines deeply, you cover most of the work.
- The number one problem EMs solve is growing and retaining engineering talent (21.4% of management scenarios). Not glamorous - it is reducing attrition, building career ladders, and creating environments where engineers do their best work.
- Cross-functional partnership is universal. Every domain (platform, product, infrastructure, data) has the same challenge: aligning engineering execution with business and product priorities.

### Skills that matter

- Hiring and talent development are mandatory (76.3%). After that: technical strategy (41.2%), delivery management (38.9%), cross-functional leadership (35.6%), system design (29.1%), stakeholder management (27.4%).
- Deep hands-on coding is overhyped for most EM roles. Only 8.1% of roles focus on writing code as a primary responsibility. 71.2% do not mention coding at all. Focus on people leadership and delivery execution first.
- 58.7% still require technical depth - but it is architectural judgment (system design trade-offs, tech debt strategy, platform decisions), not writing production code daily.

### What actually gets you hired

- People leadership is the differentiator. 62.1% of EM roles explicitly require hiring, coaching, and performance management skills. Anyone can manage a sprint board - companies hire people who can build, grow, and retain high-performing teams.
- Delivery thinking wins over technical obsession. 38.9% of EM roles require delivery management skills (project planning, risk mitigation, stakeholder communication, cross-team coordination).
- 94.8% of roles are applied management, not pure technical leadership. Only 5.2% are pure technical lead roles. The market wants people who lead through others, not people who want to keep writing code with a manager title.
