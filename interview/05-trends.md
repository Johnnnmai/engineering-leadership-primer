# Interview Trends (2026)

Emerging patterns in engineering management interviews based on candidate experiences, job descriptions, and industry discussions.


## Market Data (2025)

- EM roles grew 18% YoY as companies restructured engineering organizations post-layoffs
- Demand for EMs with AI/ML team leadership experience surged - companies building AI products need managers who understand the technical landscape
- The "player-coach" EM role is declining at top companies - they want pure people leaders, not ICs with reports
- Return to office mandates increased demand for in-person management skills (team-building, whiteboard collaboration, in-person 1:1s)


## Patterns from Practitioners

Based on 75+ interview rounds reported by engineering managers across levels:

- People management is now the primary interview focus. Three to four years ago, EM interviews were heavily technical. Now, 60-70% of interview time is spent on people scenarios
- Three interview types dominate: people management (hiring, coaching, performance), technical strategy (architecture at the leadership level), and behavioral/leadership (values, influence, conflict)
- System design shifted from "design Twitter" to "you are the EM for a team building X - how do you structure the team, manage the technical decisions, and handle the operational concerns?"
- Management presentations are common: candidates present a past team transformation or delivery recovery, then get grilled on decisions, trade-offs, and what they would change
- In-person final rounds are becoming standard for EM roles due to the inherently interpersonal nature of the work


## Realistic Assessment Interviews

A growing number of companies reject traditional management scenario questions in favor of realistic work simulations:

- Collaborative problem-solving: work through a real management scenario with the interviewer
- Role-play exercises: simulate a difficult 1:1 conversation or performance review
- Team design exercises: given a product problem, design the team structure and delivery plan
- Incident simulations: walk through an outage scenario and demonstrate incident leadership

For candidates, this means preparation should focus on demonstrating real management judgment rather than memorizing management frameworks.


## The Bar Feels Higher

Because management books and frameworks are widely available, companies now test for depth:
- Specific examples over generic principles
- Demonstrating management judgment in ambiguous situations
- Showing how you adapted your approach when your first strategy failed
- Evidence of growing and retaining engineers, not just managing them

The philosophy shift: "Can you describe a management framework?" is no longer enough. The question is now "Can you lead a team through ambiguity, grow individual engineers, and ship through others?"


## AI Tools and EM Interviews

- AI cheating is less prevalent in EM interviews because the focus on specific personal experiences makes AI-assisted answers easy to detect
- Some companies use AI-proctored early screens for EM roles, but the people management and behavioral rounds are always human-led
- EMs are increasingly expected to articulate how AI changes engineering team dynamics - team composition, skill development, delivery velocity


## Junior vs. Senior EM Expectations Are Diverging

- First-time EM: Focus on people management fundamentals, willingness to learn, and technical credibility
- Mid-level EM: Track record of hiring, performance management, and delivery across complex projects
- Senior EM / Director: Org design thinking, multi-team coordination, executive communication, and strategic technical decisions

At senior levels, EM interviews are about organizational impact, not individual team management.
