# Management Case Studies

Management case studies test your ability to analyze complex leadership scenarios and make decisions under ambiguity. Unlike behavioral questions (which test past experience), case studies test how you think through new situations.

Some companies use these as take-home exercises; others present them live during the interview. A growing number of companies use case study presentations where you prepare materials and defend your approach.


## Format

Two main formats:

- Live case study (45-90 min): the interviewer presents a management scenario and you work through it together, with follow-up questions that add complexity
- Take-home case study (2-4 hours of work, 1-2 day deadline): analyze a management scenario and prepare a written response or presentation, then defend it in a 45-60 min follow-up interview


## Case Study Scenarios

### Team Restructuring

You are the EM for a team of 10 engineers. Due to a strategic pivot, your team needs to split into two teams - one focused on the new product direction and one maintaining the existing product. Walk through:

- How do you decide who goes on which team?
- How do you communicate the change to the team?
- How do you maintain morale during the transition?
- How do you handle an engineer who does not want to work on the maintenance team?
- How do you set up the new team for success in the first 90 days?


### Incident Response

Your team's production service went down for 4 hours during peak traffic, affecting 500K users. The root cause was a deployment that bypassed the standard rollout process. Walk through:

- How do you handle the incident in real-time (communication, coordination, resolution)?
- How do you run the post-mortem?
- What systemic changes do you propose to prevent recurrence?
- How do you handle the engineer who made the deployment decision?
- How do you communicate the incident and remediation to leadership and customers?


### Team Turnaround

You are hired as the new EM for a team with high attrition (35% last year), low morale, and consistently missed deadlines. The VP gives you 6 months to turn it around. Walk through:

- How do you assess the situation in the first 2 weeks?
- What are your priorities in the first 30, 60, and 90 days?
- How do you rebuild trust with a team that has lost confidence in management?
- How do you handle the discovery that the previous EM was a major cause of the problems?
- How do you measure whether the turnaround is working?


### Delivery Recovery

Your team committed to delivering a major feature by end of quarter. With 4 weeks left, you realize the team is 6 weeks behind. Walk through:

- How do you assess the gap and identify the root causes?
- What scope trade-offs do you propose?
- How do you communicate the situation to product and leadership?
- How do you maintain team morale while pushing for delivery?
- What process changes do you make to prevent this from happening again?


### Hiring Sprint

You are building a new team from scratch. You have budget for 6 engineers and 3 months to build the team. Walk through:

- How do you define the roles and levels you need?
- How do you design the interview process?
- How do you source candidates and build a diverse pipeline?
- How do you prioritize your first hires?
- How do you onboard multiple engineers simultaneously?


### Cross-Team Conflict

Two teams you manage are in conflict. Team A built a shared service that Team B depends on, but Team A is not prioritizing Team B's requests. Both teams are frustrated. Walk through:

- How do you diagnose the root cause of the conflict?
- How do you facilitate a resolution between the teams?
- What structural changes would you propose to prevent recurring conflicts?
- How do you handle the situation if the tech leads of both teams are unable to work together?


### Budget and Headcount

Your VP asks you to reduce your team from 12 to 9 engineers due to budget cuts. Walk through:

- How do you decide which roles to eliminate?
- How do you communicate the changes to affected and remaining team members?
- How do you redistribute work across the smaller team?
- How do you maintain team morale and prevent flight risk among remaining engineers?
- How do you communicate scope changes to product partners?


## What Interviewers Look For

- Structured approach. Start with assessment, then diagnosis, then action plan, then measurement.
- Stakeholder awareness. Consider how decisions affect engineers, product partners, leadership, and customers.
- Trade-off reasoning. There are no perfect solutions in management - show that you understand what you are giving up.
- People-centered decisions. Even in technical or delivery scenarios, the best EMs consider the human impact.
- Measurement. How do you know if your approach is working? What metrics would you track?
